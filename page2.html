About This Website

We live in a world that is more connected than ever before. Smartphones, laptops, tablets and televisions have become central to how we communicate, learn, work and entertain ourselves. For many people, particularly young people growing up in the digital age, screens are not just a tool, they are a way of life.

Over the past decade, the average daily screen time across all age groups has risen significantly. Studies suggest that teenagers now spend anywhere between seven and nine hours a day looking at screens, not including time spent on schoolwork. Social media platforms, streaming services and online games are built with one goal in mind and that is to keep you engaged for as long as possible. Every notification, every like, every autoplay video is designed to pull you back in... and it's working.

But while technology has undeniably improved our lives in countless ways, this level of dependency comes at a cost. Researchers and health professionals around the world are increasingly raising concerns about the impact of excessive screen time on both mental and physical health. From anxiety and depression to eye strain, poor posture and disrupted sleep, the effects are wide ranging and often go unnoticed until they become a serious problem.

This is particularly concerning for young people, who are not only the heaviest users of digital technology but are also at a critical stage of their physical and psychological development. The habits formed now will shape their relationship with technology for years to come.

Why This Matters

Screen time is not just a personal issue — it is a public health concern. Governments, schools and health organisations across the world are beginning to take it seriously. The World Health Organisation has issued guidelines on screen time for children and young people, and mental health charities such as Mind and YoungMinds have highlighted the strong links between heavy social media use and declining mental health in teenagers. This is not about demonising technology, it is about understanding it and using it in a way that does not come at the expense of our health.

Who This Website Is For

This website is for anyone who uses screens in their daily life, but is especially aimed at young people aged 13 to 25. This age group is among the heaviest users of digital devices and is also the most vulnerable to the negative effects of excessive screen time. Whether you are a student, a parent or simply someone curious about the topic, this website aims to inform and raise awareness in a clear and accessible way.

Our Goal

This website does not exist to tell you to put your phone down and never pick it up again. The goal is simply to give you the information you need to make better choices. Screen time is not inherently bad, it is how we use it and how much of it we consume that matters. By understanding the effects it can have, you are already taking the first step towards a healthier and more balanced relationship with technology.
