How Screen Time Affects Your Mental Health

In today's digital world, screens are a huge part of everyday life, but spending too much time on them can take a serious toll on your mental health. From scrolling through social media late at night to constantly checking notifications, excessive screen time has been linked to rising levels of anxiety, depression, FOMO (Fear of Missing Out), and poor sleep, especially in young people. Understanding these effects is the first step to building a healthy relationship with technology.

Anxiety

It's easy to fall into the habit of constantly checking your phone - waiting for replies, refreshing feeds, keeping up with notifications. But this cycle of checking and waiting puts your brain under a low level of stress that builds up over time. Brain scans have confirmed that excessive gaming or screen time use can trigger dopamine releases similar to substance addiction, often leading to a cycle of dependency.

  1. Dopamine Overstimulation

    Whether it is social media or video games, many social media apps are designed to trigger the brain's reward system. Each notification or like releases dopamine, a neurotransmitter linked to reward. Frequent dopamine spikes can make the brain crave constant stimulation and when stimulation stops, dopamine levels drop, which can create restlessness. Over time, the brain may become less sensitive to normal rewards, increasing stress and anxiety.

  2. Activation of the Stress System (HPA Axis)

    Screen use can activate the body's stress response. This happens when the brain perceives conflict or information overload. This activates the hypothalamus-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. As a result, the body releases cortisol, a stress hormone. Chronically elevated cortisol is linked with increased anxiety and difficulty relaxing.

  3. Sleep Disruption from Blue Light

    Perhaps you may have heard about screens emitting blue light. Blue light affects the sleep hormone melatonin that controls sleep. Less melatonin results in poor sleep and poor sleep results in higher anxiety risk. Sleep deprivation biologically leads to increased amygdala reactivity (the brain's threat detector). Thus, the brain becomes more sensitive to perceived threats, which can increase anxious feelings.

Depression

Social media often shows the highlights of people's lives — holidays, achievements, celebrations, and exciting events. When people compare these carefully selected moments to their own everyday lives, it can create the feeling that they are falling short or missing out. Over time, this constant comparison can negatively affect self-esteem and has been linked to low mood and depression, particularly in young people.

Psychologically, this happens because the brain naturally engages in social comparison, a process where we evaluate ourselves by comparing our lives, success, and appearance to others. On social media, however, the comparison is often unrealistic because most people only share their best moments rather than their struggles or ordinary daily experiences.

Biologically, these comparisons can influence the brain's reward system, which is controlled by chemicals such as dopamine. When people receive likes, comments, or positive feedback online, the brain releases dopamine, creating a feeling of pleasure or validation. However, when someone sees others receiving more attention or appearing more successful, it can lead to feelings of rejection, inadequacy, or loneliness.

Frequent exposure to these comparisons can also increase stress hormones such as cortisol, especially if someone feels pressure to present a perfect life online. Over time, this combination of social comparison, reduced self-esteem, and emotional stress can contribute to symptoms linked with depression, such as persistent sadness, low motivation, and withdrawal from activities.

Balancing screen time, limiting social media use, and focusing on real-life relationships and activities can help reduce these negative effects on mental health.

FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)

FOMO is the unsettling feeling that others are experiencing things that you're missing out on. Social media makes this worse by constantly showing you what everyone else is doing. This often leads to compulsive phone checking in an attempt to feel connected. This deepens the feeling of exclusion. Research found that excessive screen time was positively predicted FoMO and negatively predicted life satisfaction. FoMO positively predicted sleep disturbance, while sleep disturbance negatively predicted life satisfaction. This constant checking keeps the brain in a state of alertness, making it harder to relax or focus on the present moment. Over time, this can increase anxiety and make people feel less satisfied with their own experiences.

Sleep

Using screens late at night interferes with your body's natural process. The blue light emitted by devices suppresses melatonin, which makes it harder to fall asleep and reduces the amount of quality sleep you get. Over time, poor sleep feeds back into anxiety and low mood, making matters worse. Poor sleep also affects how the brain regulates emotions. When the brain is tired, areas involved in emotional control become less effective, which can make people feel more irritable, stressed, or emotionally overwhelmed the next day.
