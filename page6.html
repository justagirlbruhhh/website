How Screen Time Affects Your Physical Health

Most people are aware of how screen time can affect their mood, but the physical effects are often overlooked. The reality is that our bodies aren't designed to sit still for hours at a time, staring at a bright screen. Over time, this can cause problems that can range from persistent headaches and back pain to disrupted sleep and reduced physical activity. We will be exploring how excessive screen time affects your body, and why it's important to take it seriously.

Eye Strain

Staring at screens for long periods without breaks causes digital eye strain. Symptoms include dry eyes, blurred vision and headaches. This happens because we blink far less when looking at screen than we normally would.

Blinking spreads a thin layer of tears across the surface of the eye called the tear film. This tear film keeps the eye moist, provides oxygen and nutrients to the cornea, and protects it from irritation. When screen use reduces our blink rate, the tear film evaporates more quickly, causing dryness and inflammation on the surface of the eye.

Focusing on a screen for a long time also forces the ciliary muscles inside the eye to remain contracted. These muscles control the shape of the lens so we can focus on nearby objects. When they stay contracted for extended periods, the muscles become fatigued, which can cause blurred vision and difficulty refocusing on objects at different distances.

In addition, screens emit large amounts of blue light, a high-energy wavelength of visible light. While small amounts are normal, prolonged exposure can increase visual fatigue because blue light scatters more easily in the eye. This makes it harder for the retina to process images clearly, causing the eyes to work harder and contributing to headaches and discomfort.

Over time, this constant strain on the eye muscles and surface tissues can lead to persistent irritation, reduced visual comfort, and difficulty concentrating during tasks that require prolonged screen use.

Posture & Back/Neck Pain

You might've noticed that you often look down at your phone or slouch forward when using a computer or tablet. This posture places excessive pressure on the spine, especially in the neck and upper back. Physiotherapists often refer to this problem as "tech neck,” a term used to describe the neck and shoulder pain caused by frequent screen use in a hunched position.

The human head weighs around 4-5 kg when it is balanced directly above the spine. However, when you tilt your head forward to look down at a screen, the effective weight placed on the neck increases dramatically. At a 45-60° angle, the pressure on the cervical spine can feel several times heavier than the head's normal weight. This forces the muscles, ligaments, and joints in the neck to work much harder to support the head.

Over time, this constant strain can cause muscle fatigue, stiffness, and inflammation in the neck and shoulders. The upper back muscles may tighten while chest muscles shorten from prolonged slouching, which can gradually affect posture. The spinal discs between vertebrae may also experience increased compression, which contributes to discomfort or chronic pain if the posture is maintained for long periods.

Long sessions of screen use also reduce natural movement. Normally, the body shifts position frequently, allowing muscles to relax and blood to circulate properly. Sitting still while focusing on a screen can limit this movement, which may lead to stiffness in the back, shoulders, and neck. Taking regular breaks, adjusting screen height to eye level, and maintaining an upright sitting position can help reduce the physical strain caused by prolonged device use.

Sleep

We already discussed how the blue light emitted by devices suppresses melatonin, the hormone responsible for sleep, which makes it harder to fall asleep and reduces the amount of quality sleep you get. But what are the implications of lack of sleep on our physical health? Here is a list of health risks:

  • Obesity
  • Diabetes
  • Heart disease
  • Mood disorders
  • Hormonal changes
  • Weakened immune system
  • Reduced life expectancy

Sedentary Behaviour

Excessive screen time often replaces physical activity, leading to a more sedentary lifestyle. This comes with various health risks, such as:

  • Obesity
  • Heart problems, such as coronary heart disease and heart attack
  • High blood pressure
  • High cholesterol
  • Stroke
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Certain cancers, including colon, breast and uterine cancers
  • Osteoporosis and falls
  • Metabolic syndrome
  • Increased feelings of depression and anxiety
